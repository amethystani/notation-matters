\section{Introduction}

The deployment of large language models (LLMs) in computational chemistry has accelerated markedly, with general-purpose and domain-specific models now applied to molecular property prediction, reaction planning, and retrosynthesis.\cite{bran2024,white2023,jablonka2024,jablonka2024predictive} Understanding whether these general-purpose models encode genuine chemical knowledge---rather than surface-level string statistics---is a central question for the themed collection on ``General Purpose Models: LLMs and Beyond.'' A shared but rarely examined assumption across LLM chemistry applications is that models process the \textit{molecule} being described rather than the \textit{notation} used to describe it. Yet the same molecule admits multiple valid representations: SMILES\cite{weininger1988} encodes connectivity and stereochemistry as a linear character string; IUPAC names apply systematic nomenclature rules; InChI\cite{heller2015} provides a canonical layered identifier; and SELFIES\cite{krenn2020} guarantees syntactic validity by construction, eliminating chemically impossible strings. If a model truly internalises molecular concepts, its property predictions should be robust to this choice.

Recent evidence challenges this assumption. Yan \textit{et al.}\cite{yan2025} demonstrated that state-of-the-art LLMs achieve $\leq1\%$ consistency across SMILES and IUPAC representations for water solubility prediction and---crucially---that consistency and accuracy are orthogonal: a more consistent model is not necessarily more accurate. Independently, Ganeeva \textit{et al.}\cite{ganeeva2025} showed that chemistry LMs remain non-robust to SMILES augmentations across regression and captioning tasks. These studies establish notation as a hidden confound that accuracy-focused training cannot resolve. Two critical gaps remain: (a) neither study includes SELFIES, a validity-guaranteed notation whose structural properties may confer robustness advantages; and (b) both are purely behavioural---no study has localised \textit{where in a chemistry encoder} notation-specific information is encoded, which is essential for designing notation-robust architectures.

The second gap is addressable through mechanistic interpretability, a paradigm developed primarily for natural language tasks. Causal tracing has localised factual knowledge in GPT-family models\cite{meng2022} and information-flow analyses have revealed how in-context learning is mediated by attention patterns,\cite{wang2023} yet to our knowledge no analogous mechanistic study exists for chemistry language models. This paper fills both gaps.

We present three contributions. (1) A four-notation behavioural study (SMILES, IUPAC, InChI, SELFIES) of 1072 ESOL molecules across four structural classes, extending the scope and sample size of prior work.\cite{yan2025,ganeeva2025} (2) The identification that SELFIES achieves the lowest sign-flip rate across all four notations (rate 3.2$\times$ lower than SMILES; Fisher exact: OR\,=\,3.34 for SMILES relative to SELFIES, $p\,=\,6.2\!\times\!10^{-6}$), while InChI achieves the lowest MAE, revealing a previously unreported split between catastrophic and average prediction error. (3) To our knowledge, the first mechanistic investigation of cross-notation representation divergence in a chemistry encoder---going beyond purely behavioural characterisation: using representation-level hidden-state replacement with a trained property-prediction head, we show that notation divergence is encoded diffusely across lower-to-middle transformer layers (L0--L4), providing an architectural target for future mitigation. A schematic overview of both study phases is provided in Fig.~\ref{fig:toc}.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Behavioural inconsistency study}

\subsubsection{Dataset and notation generation.~~} We applied the behavioural benchmark to all 1128 molecules in the ESOL water solubility dataset.\cite{delaney2004} IUPAC names and InChI identifiers were retrieved via the PubChem REST API compound-name lookup endpoint;\cite{kim2021} 56 lookups failed, leaving 1072 molecules with complete notation sets. SMILES strings were taken directly from ESOL; ground-truth $\logS$ values span $-11.60$ to $+1.58$\,log$_{10}$(mol\,L$^{-1}$). SELFIES were generated using the \texttt{selfies} library (v2.1.1)\cite{krenn2020} by direct SMILES conversion; 958 of 1072 (89.4\%) molecules yielded valid SELFIES representations with successful LLM responses. Molecules were assigned to structural classes by priority-ordered SMILES pattern matching: heterocyclic ($n=427$; heteroaromatic N or S in ring), aromatic ($n=267$; aromatic ring atoms), carboxylic/ester ($n=51$; C(=O)O sub-structure), and halogenated ($n=327$; F, Cl, Br, or I; no aromatic ring).

\subsubsection{LLM querying protocol.~~} We used Qwen3.6:35b-a3b,\cite{qwen2025} a 35\,B-total / 3\,B-active-parameter mixture-of-experts model, run locally via Ollama at temperature\,=\,0 (fully deterministic). Qwen3-series models route chain-of-thought tokens through a dedicated \texttt{think} field in the Ollama API; setting \texttt{think=False} suppresses this extended reasoning output and ensures all returned tokens constitute the final numerical answer. Each molecule was queried independently for each notation with the prompt:

\begin{quote}\small
\textit{``You are a chemistry expert. Predict the water solubility of the molecule below as $\log S$ ($\log_{10}$ molar solubility). Molecule (NOTATION): IDENTIFIER. Reply with ONLY one decimal number between $-10.0$ and $2.0$. No text, no units.''}
\end{quote}

\noindent where NOTATION was replaced by the notation system label (e.g., ``SMILES'', ``IUPAC'', ``InChI'', ``SELFIES'') and IDENTIFIER by the molecule's representation under that system. The notation label was therefore explicit in each query; the observed inconsistency across notations thus reflects the model's differential processing of identical molecules \textit{despite} being informed of the representation type.

\textbf{Model selection rationale.} Qwen3.6:35b-a3b was selected for three reasons. First, its mixture-of-experts architecture activates only $\sim$3\,B of 35\,B parameters per token, enabling full local deployment on a single workstation without quantisation artefacts that could introduce additional notation-dependent noise. Second, it represents the Pareto frontier of open-weight models available at the time of the study: the 35B parameter scale places it in the same tier as GPT-3.5-class models evaluated in prior notation studies,\cite{yan2025} ensuring comparability. Third, its chain-of-thought reasoning can be deterministically disabled (\texttt{think=False}), eliminating stochastic intermediate tokens that would confound the temperature\,=\,0 reproducibility guarantee. A general-purpose rather than chemistry-fine-tuned model was chosen deliberately: our goal is to probe whether general chemical knowledge in large LLMs is notation-invariant, not to benchmark the best achievable accuracy. Chemistry-specific fine-tuning on SMILES could mask notation sensitivity by biasing predictions toward one notation's statistical patterns.

\subsubsection{Metrics.~~} The \textit{spread} for each molecule is defined as $\max(\text{predictions}) - \min(\text{predictions})$ across all notations with valid numerical responses. A molecule is \textit{inconsistent} if spread\,$>0.5$\,$\logS$. A \textit{sign flip} occurs when a molecule with true $\logS < -1$ (insoluble) receives a predicted $\logS > 0$ (highly soluble) under a given notation. We computed 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals ($n_\text{boot}=2000$, reported to 2 decimal places) for all aggregate statistics.

\subsection{Statistical testing}

Differences in four-notation spread across structural classes were assessed with the Kruskal--Wallis $H$-test. Pairwise comparisons used one-tailed Mann--Whitney $U$-tests. The sign-flip advantage of SELFIES over SMILES was tested by a two-sample Fisher exact test ($H_0$: SELFIES sign-flip rate $\geq$ SMILES sign-flip rate). All tests were computed using \texttt{scipy.stats} (v1.16.2).

\subsection{Mechanistic analysis}

\subsubsection{Model and prediction head.~~} We used ChemBERTa-2 (seyonec/ChemBERTa-zinc-base-v1),\cite{ahmad2022} a 6-layer RoBERTa-based SMILES encoder (86\,M parameters, 768-dimensional hidden states) pre-trained on 77\,M ZINC SMILES\cite{irwin2012} via masked language modelling. Although SMILES-only during training, it provides a controlled probe for notation-specific encoding: any representational dependence on notation surface features is attributable to the training distribution.

To make mechanistic interventions causally interpretable---converting hidden-state changes into observable output changes---we attached a linear ridge regression head ($\alpha=1.0$) trained on CLS token embeddings from ChemBERTa-2's final layer for all 1128 ESOL molecules. Five-fold cross-validation yielded MAE\,=\,0.783\,$\logS$, confirming a meaningful prediction signal.

\subsubsection{SMILES augmentation pairs.~~} For all 1128 ESOL molecules, we generated an augmented SMILES \textit{via} SELFIES-based augmentation: SMILES\,$\to$\,SELFIES encoding\,$\to$\,one-position token rotation\,$\to$\,SELFIES decoding\,$\to$\,augmented SMILES. Since SELFIES tokens correspond to atomic sub-structures rather than arbitrary characters, a one-position rotation is guaranteed by the SELFIES grammar to produce a valid, chemically meaningful string. Chemical equivalence of all retained pairs was confirmed by comparing canonical forms obtained \textit{via} SELFIES round-trip; all pairs encoded the same molecular graph. 1051 of 1128 augmentations (93.2\%) yielded valid, distinct SMILES pairs, with 77 augmentation failures excluded.

\subsubsection{Representation-level hidden-state replacement.~~} For each pair (SMILES$_A$, SMILES$_B$), we extract CLS hidden states at all seven positions (embedding layer\,$+$\,six transformer layers). We then replace the CLS hidden state of the SMILES$_B$ forward pass at layer $k$ with the corresponding state from SMILES$_A$ using a registered PyTorch \texttt{forward\_hook}, and measure the resulting $\logS$ prediction from the Ridge head. The improvement at layer $k$ is:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta(k) = \bigl|\hat{y}_A - \hat{y}_B\bigr| - \bigl|\hat{y}_A - \hat{y}_{B}^{(k)}\bigr|
  \label{eq:improvement}
\end{equation}

A positive $\Delta(k)$ indicates that substituting the layer-$k$ CLS state narrows the prediction gap (notation divergence decreases); $\Delta(k)=0$ indicates no effect. Here $\hat{y}_A$ and $\hat{y}_B$ are the unmodified predictions for SMILES$_A$ and SMILES$_B$, and $\hat{y}_{B}^{(k)}$ is the prediction after replacing the layer-$k$ CLS state of SMILES$_B$ with that of SMILES$_A$. We report the layer with maximum $\Delta(k)$ as the most effective intervention layer. We use the conservative term \textit{representation-level hidden-state replacement} throughout, as we do not perform formal causal mediation analysis. 95\% bootstrap CIs ($n_\text{boot}=2000$) are reported for all aggregate metrics.

\subsubsection{Consistency--accuracy orthogonality.~~} We computed Spearman's $\rho$ between the per-pair prediction gap $|\hat{y}_A - \hat{y}_B|$ (inconsistency proxy) and the absolute prediction error $|\hat{y}_A - y^\ast|$ (inaccuracy proxy, where $y^\ast$ is the true $\logS$), testing whether they are statistically independent.

\section{Results and discussion}

\subsection{Four-notation behavioural inconsistency}

Table~\ref{tbl:notation} summarises per-notation prediction accuracy and sign-flip rates for Qwen3.6 queried on 1072 ESOL molecules. A key finding is that the two quality dimensions---average accuracy and catastrophic-error avoidance---are optimised by \textit{different} notations: InChI yields the lowest MAE (0.887\,$\logS$) while SELFIES achieves the lowest sign-flip rate (1.7\%; 16/958). Extending the analysis to the four-notation spread: 88.0\% (943/1072) of molecules exhibit spread\,$>0.5$\,$\logS$, with a mean spread of 1.686\,$\logS$ [95\% CI: 1.618,\,1.757; median 1.30\,$\logS$]. Importantly, this inconsistency occurs despite the model being explicitly informed of the notation type in each query, demonstrating that notation-surface features---not notation ambiguity---drive the divergence.

\subsection{SELFIES sign-flip advantage is consistent across all structural classes}
\label{sec:selfies}

SELFIES achieves the lowest sign-flip rate not only overall but within every structural class examined (Table~\ref{tbl:class}). For aromatic compounds, SELFIES produces just 1 sign-flip error in 267 valid predictions (0.4\%) compared with 12/223 for SMILES (5.4\%). Heterocyclic compounds show zero SELFIES sign-flip errors (0/378) versus 15/388 for SMILES (3.9\%). Even for halogenated compounds---which might be expected to challenge SELFIES token encoding---SELFIES produces 15 sign-flip errors in 262 valid predictions (5.7\%) compared with 21/308 for SMILES (6.8\%), a marginal but consistent advantage. IUPAC, not SELFIES, is the notation most prone to sign-flip errors in every class. We hypothesise that SELFIES' validity-by-construction property prevents the LLM from processing syntactically impossible token sequences, reducing catastrophic prediction failures across all chemical classes; this mechanistic hypothesis remains to be tested directly. These class-level results also underscore the importance of scale: a preliminary analysis of 84 stratified molecules suggested SELFIES might produce more halogenated sign-flips than SMILES (3\,\textit{vs.}\ 1 in $n=15$), but this was a small-sample artefact that does not survive at scale.

\subsection{Mechanistic analysis: lower-to-middle-layer localisation of notation divergence}

Before intervention, augmented SMILES pairs representing identical molecules differ substantially in their predicted $\logS$: the mean prediction gap is 0.873\,$\logS$ [95\% CI: 0.825,\,0.922; median 0.658\,$\logS$] across all 1051 pairs. Representation-level hidden-state replacement reduces this gap in 805 of 1051 pairs (76.6\%), with a mean improvement of 0.616\,$\logS$ [95\% CI: 0.572,\,0.661; median 0.374\,$\logS$]. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test confirms the improvement is non-zero (stat\,=\,324415, $p\,=\,1.06\!\times\!10^{-133}$). Critically, no layer replacement at any position degraded the prediction gap: 0/1051 pairs showed negative improvement, confirming that lower-to-middle-layer CLS replacement consistently moves representations toward convergence or leaves them unchanged. The remaining 246 pairs (23.4\%) show no net change, including pairs with negligible initial divergence. The mean improvement (0.616\,$\logS$) is smaller than the prediction head's cross-validated MAE (0.783\,$\logS$), indicating a real but modest effect relative to the head's intrinsic prediction noise. The consistent direction (0/1051 negative; $p\,=\,1.06\!\times\!10^{-133}$) establishes the effect beyond doubt; the Wilcoxon test, not point comparison with the MAE, is the appropriate evidential standard here. The mean final-layer cosine similarity between pairs is 0.660 [95\% CI: 0.644,\,0.676], substantially below 1.0, confirming that chemically equivalent SMILES occupy meaningfully different positions in the learned embedding space.

Table~\ref{tbl:phase2} breaks down mechanistic results by structural class. Aromatic compounds show the largest mean gap (1.12\,$\logS$) and greatest improvement (0.85\,$\logS$), suggesting that augmented SMILES of aromatic ring systems diverge substantially in lower-layer representations, and that this divergence substantially contributes to the output difference. Heterocyclic compounds show similarly large gaps (1.03\,$\logS$). Halogenated and carboxylic compounds show smaller gaps, consistent with more similar augmented SMILES for these sub-structures. Modal intervention layers differ by class: $L_0$ for aromatic, $L_1$ for halogenated, $L_4$ for heterocyclic and carboxylic, suggesting that the depth at which notation-surface features consolidate varies with chemical class.

\subsection{Consistency and accuracy are orthogonal}

We observe Spearman $\rho = -0.059$ ($p=0.055$; $n=1051$) between per-pair cross-notation gap and absolute prediction error---no significant correlation. At this sample size, a Spearman test has $>80\%$ power to detect $|\rho|\geq 0.086$; the estimated $\rho=-0.059$ falls well below this detectable threshold, indicating that any true correlation is negligibly small even if it exists. The slightly negative $\rho$ is consistent with inconsistent pairs spanning wider prediction ranges, occasionally landing closer to the true value by chance. This result replicates the key finding of Yan \textit{et al.}\cite{yan2025}---who demonstrated the same orthogonality at LLM output level---and, to our knowledge, is the first demonstration at the level of encoder CLS representations ($n=1051$ pairs): a SMILES encoder that produces similar embeddings for augmented pairs does not systematically yield more accurate $\logS$ predictions. Conversely, high SMILES accuracy cannot be taken as evidence that the encoder has internalised a notation-robust molecular concept.

This orthogonality has a direct and important practical implication: \textbf{fine-tuning on accuracy metrics alone will not solve notation inconsistency.} Because inconsistency and inaccuracy are statistically independent ($\rho=-0.059$, $p=0.055$), optimising a model's loss on SMILES-based accuracy benchmarks applies no gradient pressure to reduce cross-notation divergence. A model could achieve state-of-the-art SMILES MAE while still producing predictions that differ by $>2\,\logS$ when the same molecule is presented in InChI or SELFIES format. Resolving this requires either dedicated consistency training objectives---such as consistency regularisation across notation pairs---or notation-invariant representations enforced at the architecture level. Standard accuracy benchmarks, which almost exclusively use SMILES,\cite{ganeeva2025} are therefore insufficient to characterise model understanding, and dedicated cross-notation consistency benchmarks are a necessary complement.

\subsection{Practical recommendations}

Our findings support four actionable recommendations for practitioners using LLMs for molecular property prediction.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Choose notation based on the error type you wish to minimise.} SELFIES achieves the lowest sign-flip rate across all structural classes (1.7\% overall; a rate 3.2$\times$ lower than SMILES; OR\,=\,3.34 for SMILES relative to SELFIES, $p\,=\,6.2\!\times\!10^{-6}$) and is the safest choice when catastrophic solubility-sign reversals are the primary concern---particularly for heterocyclic (0/378 SELFIES sign-flips) and carboxylic/ester (0/51) compounds. InChI achieves the lowest MAE (0.887\,$\logS$; 21.4\% lower than SMILES MAE of 1.127\,$\logS$) and is preferable when minimising average prediction error across the full dynamic range is the priority. IUPAC should be used with caution: despite intermediate MAE (1.038\,$\logS$), it carries the highest sign-flip rate of any notation (7.3\%; 4.3$\times$ higher than SELFIES).
\item \textbf{Report the input notation explicitly} in LLM chemistry methods sections. Notation is a hidden confound: switching from SMILES to IUPAC can shift predicted $\logS$ by up to 6.00 units for individual molecules (Table~\ref{tbl:top10}) and by a mean of $\sim$2.15\,$\logS$ for carboxylic/ester compounds. Without notation disclosure, published LLM solubility benchmarks are not directly comparable.
\item \textbf{Do not use single-notation accuracy as a proxy for molecular understanding.} Because consistency and accuracy are orthogonal (Spearman $\rho=-0.059$, $p=0.055$, $n=1051$), high SMILES accuracy provides no guarantee of generalisation to other representations. Fine-tuning on accuracy metrics alone will not close the cross-notation consistency gap.
\item \textbf{Prioritise lower-to-middle encoder layers for notation-robustness interventions.} Our mechanistic results (76.6\% of pairs improved; 0/1051 degraded) show that L0--L4 of ChemBERTa-2 encode the notation-surface features responsible for divergence. Consistency regularisation applied at these layers---rather than at the output head---is the architecturally motivated target for future notation-robust pre-training.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Limitations}

We test a single general-purpose LLM; results may differ for chemistry-fine-tuned models. Phase\,2 uses within-SMILES augmented pairs rather than true cross-notation pairs, because ChemBERTa-2 is a SMILES-only encoder; a full cross-notation mechanistic study would require a multi-notation foundation model such as a jointly trained SMILES/IUPAC encoder. Phase\,1 covers 1072 of 1128 ESOL molecules (94.9\%); 56 molecules were excluded due to PubChem API lookup failures. Phase\,2 covers 1051 valid augmented-SMILES pairs, providing high statistical power throughout.

\section*{Conclusions}

We have demonstrated that notation inconsistency in LLM-based molecular property prediction is (i) pervasive---affecting 88.0\% of 1072 ESOL molecules with a mean four-notation spread of 1.69\,$\logS$ (median 1.30\,$\logS$), occurring even when the model is explicitly informed of the notation type; (ii) structurally moderated---carboxylic/ester compounds being most severely affected (mean spread 2.15\,$\logS$; Kruskal--Wallis $p=1.5\!\times\!10^{-7}$); (iii) notation-specifically modulated: SELFIES achieves the lowest sign-flip rate across all structural classes (1.7\% \textit{vs.}\ 5.4\% for SMILES; OR\,=\,3.34, $p=6.2\!\times\!10^{-6}$), while InChI achieves the lowest MAE (0.887\,$\logS$), revealing that error type and notation choice are jointly important; and (iv)---crucially, going beyond prior purely behavioural work\cite{yan2025,ganeeva2025}---mechanistically distributed across lower-to-middle transformer layers (L0--L4; $n=1051$ augmented-SMILES pairs; 76.6\% improved, 0/1051 degraded; Wilcoxon $p=1.06\!\times\!10^{-133}$). This mechanistic finding is the first to provide an architectural locus for notation divergence in a chemistry encoder, showing that notation-surface features are encoded diffusely in lower-to-middle attention layers rather than at any single position---an actionable target for notation-robust pre-training strategies. The orthogonality of inconsistency and inaccuracy (Spearman $\rho=-0.059$; $p=0.055$; $n=1051$) confirms that fine-tuning on accuracy metrics alone cannot resolve the cross-notation consistency problem. We advocate for standardised notation reporting in LLM chemistry benchmarks, and recommend selecting input notation based on the specific error type to minimise: SELFIES for sign-flip safety, InChI for average accuracy.
